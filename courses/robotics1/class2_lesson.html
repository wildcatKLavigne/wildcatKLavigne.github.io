Part 1  ·  The Engineering Design Process (15 min)

A Loop, Not a Line

The Engineering Design Process (EDP) is the structured approach engineers use to solve problems. It is not a recipe you follow once — it is a loop. When you test something and it doesn't perform the way you expected, that's not failure. That is data. You use the data to loop back, understand what went wrong, and improve.

The six stages are: Define the problem (what must the solution do, and what are the constraints?) → Research (what do others know? what materials and methods exist?) → Ideate (sketch at least 3 different solutions — never just one) → Prototype (build a testable version) → Test & Measure (collect real data) → Iterate (use what you learned to improve the design and loop back).

Today you will run through the full EDP loop three times in a single class period using a constrained design challenge. Each loop gives you data. Each loop gives you a chance to improve. By the third attempt your tower should be meaningfully better than the first — not because you got lucky, but because you designed smarter.

Part 2  ·  Challenge Setup: Define & Research (8 min)

Stage 1 & 2 — Define the Problem & Research

The Challenge: Using exactly one sheet of standard 8.5"×11" paper and up to 30 cm (about 12") of clear tape, build the tallest freestanding tower you can. The tower must stand on its own for at least 10 seconds to count. You will have three attempts — one sheet of paper per attempt, building time limited per round.

Constraints: One sheet of paper per attempt. Up to 30 cm of tape total per attempt. No other materials. Tower must be freestanding (nothing touching it, no leaning against anything). Must survive a 10-second count after your hands leave it.

I Do Watch the teacher model the Define and Research stages

Defining the problem: "Build the tallest freestanding paper tower using one 8.5"×11" sheet and up to 30 cm of tape. Success = stands for 10 seconds unassisted. Key constraints: paper area is fixed, tape is limited, no external support."

Researching what I know about structures: "Tall structures stay up when their base is wider than their top (pyramidal geometry). Cylinders and tubes are stronger than flat sheets because the curved form resists buckling. Triangles in a structure resist deformation better than rectangles. The taller something gets, the heavier the top becomes relative to the base — weight at the top causes tipping."

The key insight from research: "Before I sketch any design, I know two things: (1) I need to transform the flat paper into a structural shape — a flat sheet has almost no strength. (2) The base needs to be as wide and stable as possible. These two insights will drive every sketch I produce."

You Do Part 2A — Your Define & Research

Before sketching anything, record what you already know about paper structures and what constraints will most limit your design.

Part 3  ·  Stage 3 — Ideate: Three Designs (10 min)

Generate Three Different Approaches

Before touching the paper, sketch three completely different tower architectures. Each sketch must show how the paper is shaped, where the base is, and how the tower stays upright. Label key features. Record one predicted advantage and one predicted weakness for each.

I Do Watch the teacher model the Ideate stage — three different designs

How I generate three genuinely different ideas: "I don't let my first idea block me. My first instinct might be 'roll it into a tube.' That's fine — I sketch it. But then I ask: what is a completely different structural approach? What if the base was the feature, not the height? What if I folded it accordion-style? What if I made a cone? Each sketch should use the paper differently."

Design A — Cylinder (rolled tube): Roll the full sheet into a vertical tube. Advantage: tubes resist vertical crushing (column strength). Weakness: the base is only as wide as the tube diameter — may tip easily.

Design B — Fan-fold accordion: Fold paper in tight accordion folds to create a stiff corrugated column. Advantage: multiple panels share the load; very resistant to buckling. Weakness: limited height because folding uses paper length.

Design C — Wide tripod base with tall mast: Cut/fold the lower third into three outward-spreading base legs; roll the remaining upper portion into a narrow tube for height. Advantage: very stable base; separates stability from height concerns. Weakness: the connection point between legs and mast is a single failure point.

My selection and why: "I'll try Design C first because the tripod base solves my biggest concern (tipping) and gives me the most height potential. But I keep A and B in mind — if C fails, I know exactly what to try next."

You Do Part 3A — Your Three Design Sketches

Sketch three genuinely different tower designs. Each sketch should show how the paper is folded/shaped and where the base sits. Label key features. Then evaluate and select one to build first.
