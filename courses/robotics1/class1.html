Part 1  ·  Reading & Discussion (15 min)

What Makes a Design Purposeful?

Engineering is the practice of solving problems with constraints. A constraint is a limit you must work within — weight, cost, size, time, available materials. Engineers don't just build whatever they imagine; they make decisions that balance competing demands.

Purposeful design means every choice in your robot has a reason. You chose that wheel size because it gives the right ground clearance. You placed the battery low because it lowers the center of gravity. You used a modular bracket so you can swap it later. When someone asks "why did you build it that way?" you should have an answer.

Trial-and-error means trying things until something works — without understanding why it works. It can get you a functioning robot, but you won't be able to improve it reliably or explain it to a judge, a teammate, or next year's team.

After the class discussion, answer these questions in the space below.

1. In your own words, what is the difference between purposeful design and trial-and-error?

2. Give one example from everyday life (not a robot) where someone used purposeful design. What was the constraint they were working within?

Quick Experiment  ·  Stability & Weight Placement (5 min)

Where Weight Lives Changes Everything

Before analyzing robots, you need one concept in your hands: stability — how easily something tips over. This 5-minute experiment gives you the idea physically so you can apply it when analyzing robot photos.

Materials (per group)
1 empty plastic water bottle with cap  ·  1 heavy book or a few textbooks  ·  1 small flat object to place on top (eraser or phone)
Procedure

Step 1 — Baseline. Stand the empty water bottle upright on the table. Gently push it from the side with one finger. Note how much force it takes to tip it.

Step 2 — Weight on top. Balance the heavy book on top of the water bottle. Gently push from the side with the same force. What happens?

Step 3 — Weight on bottom. Now place the book flat on the table and stand the water bottle on top of it (book is the base). Push the bottle again. Compare to Step 2.

Step 4 — Predict. Before you look at any robot photos: if a robot's battery is the heaviest single part, where would you want to mount it — high or low? Why?

Record your observations:

Setup What happened when you pushed? How much force did it take to tip?
Step 1 — Bottle alone
Step 2 — Book on top
Step 3 — Book on bottom

3. Write a rule in your own words: where should the heaviest part of a robot be mounted, and why?

Key term: The point where an object's weight is balanced is called its center of gravity. When the center of gravity is low (close to the ground), the object is stable and hard to tip. When it is high, small pushes can tip it easily. In your Step 2 experiment, the book raised the center of gravity — in Step 3, the book lowered it. You will see this reasoning again when analyzing robot photos.

Reading a Robot Like an Engineer

I Do Watch the teacher model engineering thinking on this example robot

Before you analyze your own robot photo, watch your teacher think out loud using the example below. Notice how each observation leads to a "why" question, and how every design choice has a trade-off.

Example robot — annotated for discussion
Observation A — Battery placement

I notice: The battery is mounted low and centered on the frame — not up high, not off to one side.

I ask "why?": A heavy object mounted low lowers the robot's center of gravity. That makes it much harder to tip over when turning fast or climbing a ramp. Centering it left-to-right keeps the weight balanced so the robot doesn't pull to one side.

Trade-off: Mounting low takes up interior space that could have been used for wiring or a second mechanism.

Observation B — Wheel size

I notice: The robot uses 4-inch diameter wheels at all four corners, evenly spaced.

I ask "why?": Larger wheels mean the robot travels farther per motor revolution — higher top speed. Four wheels (two per side) share the driving load and reduce the chance of getting stuck. Equal spacing keeps the turning center predictable.

Trade-off: Bigger wheels raise the chassis height, which raises the center of gravity slightly. They also take more torque to accelerate from a stop.

Observation C — Arm position

I notice: The arm is mounted at the front-center of the robot, not to the side.

I ask "why?": Centering the arm keeps the robot balanced when the arm extends — if it were on one side, extending it would shift the center of mass sideways and the robot could tip or track crooked. Front-mounting means the driver just needs to point the robot at the target.

Trade-off: The arm now blocks the front camera's view when fully extended. The team would need to mount the camera higher or use a rear camera instead.

I Do Watch the teacher model a comparison paragraph using two example robots

A strong comparison paragraph doesn't just list differences — it explains what each difference reveals about the team's priorities. Watch how the teacher structures this.

Two example robots being compared

Robot A — Speed-focused design
  • 6" diameter wheels (large)
  • Battery mounted high on frame
  • No arm — only a pusher bar
  • 4-wheel drive, wide stance
Robot B — Capability-focused design
  • 4" diameter wheels (smaller)
  • Battery mounted low and centered
  • Articulated arm with gripper
  • 4-wheel drive, narrower frame

Teacher's modeled comparison paragraph

Both robots use four-wheel drive, which tells us both teams prioritized reliable traction and stability over simplicity. However, their wheel size choices reveal very different priorities: Robot A's 6-inch wheels will travel farther per motor revolution, giving it a higher top speed — suggesting this team valued crossing the field quickly. Robot B's smaller 4-inch wheels sacrifice speed for more torque, which is better suited to climbing ramps or pushing against resistance.

The most significant structural difference is battery placement. Robot A mounts the battery high, which raises the center of gravity — a risk for a fast robot making sharp turns. This suggests the team either didn't anticipate tipping as a problem, or accepted the risk to keep their frame design simple. Robot B mounts the battery low and centered, a deliberate stability choice that shows awareness of the physics involved.

Finally, Robot A has no arm — just a pusher bar — while Robot B has a full articulated gripper. This tells us Robot A was designed for a strategy that doesn't require manipulating game elements precisely, while Robot B was built for a more complex task. The trade-off is that Robot B is heavier, slower, and harder to repair under time pressure.

What made that paragraph effective?

✓ Named specific features, not vague ones ("6-inch wheels" not "big wheels")
✓ Explained what each difference reveals about team priorities
✓ Named at least one similarity before diving into differences
✓ Identified the trade-off each choice created
You Do Part 2B — Comparison Paragraph (Intermediate & Proficient)

Compare the two robot photos your teacher displays. Identify at least 2 similarities and 2 differences. For each difference, explain what it reveals about what each team prioritized. Use specific feature names and engineering vocabulary.

I Do Watch the teacher model an improvement proposal with engineering rationale

An improvement proposal isn't a complaint — it's a structured engineering argument. It names a specific problem, explains the root cause, proposes a concrete alternative, and predicts the trade-off of that alternative. Watch the structure.

Teacher's modeled improvement proposal — targeting Robot A's battery placement

Current design Robot A mounts the battery in a bracket near the top of the frame, approximately 10 inches above the ground plane.
Problem identified A high center of gravity increases the risk of tipping during fast turns or when the robot contacts a field element. At 6-inch wheel diameter and high drive speed, lateral forces during turning will be significant. A tipped robot cannot recover autonomously.
My proposed change Relocate the battery to a low-mounted tray centered between the two drive rails, 2–3 inches above the ground plane. Use a REV battery strap and quick-release bracket so it can be swapped rapidly between matches.
Trade-off of my change Low battery placement reduces interior frame space, which could interfere with wiring runs or a future mechanism. The team should plan cable routing before finalizing the tray position.

Notice the pattern: Current → Problem → Change → Trade-off. Every section is specific — no vague language.

Part 3  ·  Engineering Notebook Setup (15 min)

Your First Notebook Entry

Your engineering notebook is a legal record of your design process. In competitions like FIRST Tech Challenge, judges read notebooks to understand how your team thinks — not just what you built. Every entry must be dated and signed. Every sketch must be labeled. Every decision must have a reason.

Today you will set up your notebook and make your first entry: a title page and a definition entry for purposeful design.

Notebook setup checklist — check each box as you complete it in your physical notebook:

Notebook Entry Prompt

Write today's full date at the top right. Then write: "Today I learned that purposeful design means..." — finish this sentence in at least 3 sentences. Then sketch or describe one design choice from the robot photo and explain the engineering reason behind it.

Extension / Exploration — Purposeful Failure

Build it wrong on purpose. Using materials from today's class (paper, tape, or anything on your desk), build a small tower or structure that you deliberately design to fail in a specific way — for example, one that will definitely tip to the left, or one whose middle will buckle before the base. Before you build it: write down exactly how and where you predict it will fail. Then build it, test it, and record what actually happened.

In your engineering notebook, write 3 sentences: (1) where you predicted the failure, (2) where the failure actually occurred, and (3) what this tells you about where engineers need to look hardest for problems. Then watch the video below about the Gimli Glider (1983) — a real plane that ran out of fuel mid-flight because of a unit conversion error. Apply the same three questions to that failure.
