Part 1  ·  Reading & Discussion (15 min)

What Makes a Design Intentional?

Engineering is the practice of solving problems with constraints. A constraint is a limit you must work within — weight, cost, size, time, available materials. Engineers don't just build whatever they imagine; they make decisions that balance competing demands.

Intentional design means every choice in your robot has a reason. You chose that wheel size because it gives the right ground clearance. You placed the battery low because it lowers the center of gravity. You used a modular bracket so you can swap it later. When someone asks "why did you build it that way?" you should have an answer.

Trial-and-error means trying things until something works — without understanding why it works. It can get you a functioning robot, but you won't be able to improve it reliably or explain it to a judge, a teammate, or next year's team.

After the class discussion, answer these questions in the space below.

1. In your own words, what is the difference between intentional design and trial-and-error?

2. Give one example from everyday life (not a robot) where someone used intentional design. What was the constraint they were working within?

Part 2  ·  Robot Photo Analysis (20 min)

Reading a Robot Like an Engineer

Your teacher will show you a photo (or photos) of a REV-based robot. Examine it carefully before answering.

Novice task Identify and label 3 design choices you can see. Use the table below.
Intermediate task Complete the table AND write a comparison paragraph (Part 2B).
Proficient task Complete both tables, the paragraph, and propose one improvement with full rationale.

Part 2A — Design Decisions Table

For each design choice you observe, describe what you see, guess the reason for that choice, and identify any trade-off it might create.

Part 2B — Comparison Paragraph (Intermediate & Proficient)

If you have a second robot photo, compare the two designs. Which design choices are similar? Which are different? What does each difference tell you about what each team prioritized?

Part 2C — Improvement Proposal (Proficient)

Choose one design decision you observed that you think could be improved. Describe the current design, explain the problem, and propose a specific alternative. Use engineering vocabulary.

Part 3  ·  Engineering Notebook Setup (15 min)

Your First Notebook Entry

Your engineering notebook is a legal record of your design process. In competitions like FIRST Tech Challenge, judges read notebooks to understand how your team thinks — not just what you built. Every entry must be dated and signed. Every sketch must be labeled. Every decision must have a reason.

Today you will set up your notebook and make your first entry: a title page and a definition entry for intentional design.

Notebook setup checklist — check each box as you complete it in your physical notebook:

Notebook Entry Prompt

Write today's full date at the top right. Then write: "Today I learned that intentional design means..." — finish this sentence in at least 3 sentences. Then sketch or describe one design choice from the robot photo and explain the engineering reason behind it.

Extension / Exploration

Research a real-world engineering failure — good starting points include the Tacoma Narrows Bridge (1940), the Gimli Glider (1983), or the Therac-25 radiation machine (1985–1987). Write a 1-page analysis in your engineering notebook: What was the design intent? What constraint was underestimated or ignored? What would intentional design have caught?
