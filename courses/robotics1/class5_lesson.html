Part 1  ·  What Is Modular Design? (12 min)

Building in Sections — On Purpose

A module is a self-contained unit that has a clearly defined job, can be designed and built independently, and connects to the rest of the system through a standardized interface. Modular design is the deliberate choice to organize a system as a collection of modules rather than one unified mass.

A truly modular system has five properties: (1) Functional independence — each module does one job and does it fully. (2) Defined interfaces — the connection points between modules are standardized and documented. (3) Replaceability — you can swap one module for another without redesigning the whole system. (4) Testability — each module can be tested alone before being integrated. (5) Parallel development — different teams can build different modules at the same time because the interfaces are agreed upon in advance.

You have already seen modular design in the REV Robotics extrusion system: any REV bracket fits any REV extrusion at any point along its length. That standardized hole pattern is the interface. It means you can swap a motor mount without redesigning the frame. Today you will find the same principles in a LEGO® Technic™ set.

Class 1 connection: In intentional design, every choice has a reason. Choosing to make something modular is itself an intentional design decision — made for specific engineering reasons. Keep asking: why modular here? what problem does it solve?

Part 2  ·  Meet the Model: LEGO® Technic™ 42197 Backhoe Loader (5 min)

A 104-Piece Engineering Case Study

The LEGO® Technic™ 42197 Backhoe Loader is a 104-piece model of a real construction machine. Real backhoe loaders are used on building sites to dig trenches (with the rear arm) and to move and lift material (with the front bucket). The real machine must be able to do two very different jobs — which is why it has two distinctly different mechanisms attached to a common chassis.

The model includes four visible subsystems: a chassis and drivetrain (the frame, wheels, and axles that the machine rolls on); a front loader arm (a knob-operated arm with a bucket scoop that lifts and lowers); a rear digger arm (a multi-joint arm for digging); and outrigger stabilizers (two legs that fold out to the sides to prevent tipping when the digger is in use). Each of these is a candidate module.

I Do Watch the teacher model how to observe a modular system as an engineer

Before I pick up any part, I ask: "What is the machine supposed to do? A backhoe loader has two jobs — move material at the front, and dig at the rear. These two jobs are different enough that they need separate mechanisms. That tells me immediately that modularity was probably intentional: the designers needed a way to work on the front mechanism without redesigning the rear one."

I look for the interfaces first: "Where does one part of the machine end and another begin? On this model, the front loader arm attaches to the chassis at a single pivot axle — one connection point. The rear digger arm attaches at a different single pivot on the opposite end. The outriggers fold on pivot pins at the sides. Each of these pivot points is an interface. The interface is always simpler than the module it connects — that's a sign of good modular design."

I test functional independence: "If I removed the front loader arm completely, would the rear digger still work? Yes — because the two arms share no mechanical linkage. Each has its own mechanism driven independently. That's functional independence in action."

I look for replaceability signals: "Could LEGO redesign the bucket shape without changing the chassis? Yes — the bucket attaches at the end of the arm with a standard pin. The chassis doesn't care what shape the bucket is. That's replaceability: the module boundary isolates each part from change in the others."

Part 4  ·  Architecture Diagram & Interface Map (8 min)

Mapping the Whole System

Now that you understand each module individually, map how they fit together as a system. The architecture diagram shows every module as a box and every interface as a labeled connection line. The interface map describes what physical mechanism creates each connection.

I Do Watch the teacher draw and explain the system architecture diagram

How I draw a modular architecture diagram: "I put the chassis in the center — it's the module everything else connects to. It's the hub of the system. Then I draw the other three modules branching off the chassis: front loader arm on one side, rear digger on the other, outriggers at the sides. Each connecting line gets a label: what type of interface is it? For the 42197, all three attachments are pin-and-axle connections — that's the LEGO equivalent of a standardized interface."

What the diagram reveals: "Notice that Module 2 (front loader) and Module 3 (rear digger) do NOT connect to each other — they both connect only to the chassis. This is important: no direct coupling between the two working arms. If they were connected to each other, a change in one would force a change in the other. The chassis acts as the integration layer — the thing that holds all the modules together without letting them depend on each other."

Identifying interface types: "Every interface has a type: mechanical (physical attachment — pin, axle, bracket), power (transmitting force or motion), data/signal (in electronic systems), or structural (load-bearing). On the 42197, all interfaces are mechanical. In your REV robotics robot, you'll have all four types."

Part 6  ·  Redesign One Module (15 min)  ·  EDP + Sketching Practice

Your Turn to Improve the System

You will choose one module from the 42197 and propose an improved redesign. The constraint: your redesign must use the same interface as the original — it must connect to the chassis at the same attachment point. The module's function must remain the same or be improved. You may change the mechanism, shape, size, or operation method.

You will apply the full EDP (Class 2): Define what problem with the original module you're solving → Research what you know that could solve it → Ideate three sketched alternatives → Select one using a decision matrix → Document with a notebook-quality entry including a revision box.

I Do Watch the teacher model redesigning the front bucket using three design sketches

My identified problem: "The front bucket on the 42197 is flat-bottomed and quite shallow. This means it can push material but can't scoop and hold it — it spills out easily. My redesign goal: a bucket that retains material better without changing the attachment interface."

Design R1 — Deeper curved bucket: Top view shows same width, but side profile shows a curved interior with raised rear wall. The curved shape creates a pocket that holds material. Predicted advantage: holds material far better. Predicted weakness: deeper bucket increases weight at the end of the arm, which adds load on the pivot — may require a stiffer arm linkage.

Design R2 — Hinged clamshell bucket: Two half-buckets that close like jaws. A gear mechanism at the pivot opens and closes them. Advantage: can grab and hold discrete objects. Weakness: requires an additional gear linkage connected to a second knob — adds complexity and weight.

Design R3 — Wider shallow bucket with side wings: Same depth as original but 30% wider with curved side walls to prevent side spillage. Top view shows a U-shape rather than a rectangle. Advantage: easy to build, minimal weight increase, no mechanism change needed. Weakness: wider bucket reduces clearance and makes the machine harder to operate in narrow spaces.

My decision matrix scores: "R1: material retention=3, weight impact=2, complexity=3, interface compatibility=3. Total=11. R3: 2, 3, 3, 3. Total=11. R2: 3, 1, 1, 2. Total=7. Tie between R1 and R3. I choose R3 because complexity=3 matters most — this is an entry-level set, and I want to keep the mechanism simple."
