Part 1  ·  Skills Review & New Tool: The Weighted Decision Matrix (10 min)

From Simple to Weighted Evaluation

In Class 3, you used a simple decision matrix: rate each design 1–3 on each criterion and total the scores. This works — but it treats all criteria as equally important. In real engineering, some requirements matter more than others. A robot claw that is lightweight but can barely grip anything is worthless. A claw that grips perfectly but can't be attached to the robot is also worthless. These requirements don't have equal weight.

The weighted decision matrix adds a multiplier to each criterion: the weight. You assign each criterion a weight (1–3, where 3 = most important). You still rate each design 1–3 on that criterion. Then you multiply: rating × weight = weighted score. Total all weighted scores to get the design's overall score. The design with the highest total wins — but only if the highest-weighted requirements are truly met.

This is a direct application of purposeful design (Class 1): a weighted matrix forces you to decide what matters most before you pick a design — rather than letting the numbers accidentally decide for you.

I Do Watch the teacher model a weighted decision matrix on a simple example

Example problem: "I need to choose a material for a robot bracket: aluminum, 3D-printed PLA, or thick cardboard. Three criteria matter: strength, weight, and ease of modification."

Step 1 — Assign weights before rating: "I weight strength = 3 (most critical — if it breaks we fail), weight = 2 (lighter is better but not critical), ease of modification = 1 (nice to have but least important). I must assign weights before I rate designs — if I rate first, I'll unconsciously bias the weights toward the design I already like."

Step 2 — Rate each design: "Aluminum: strength=3, weight=1, modification=1. PLA: strength=2, weight=2, modification=2. Cardboard: strength=1, weight=3, modification=3."

Step 3 — Calculate weighted scores: "Aluminum: (3×3)+(1×2)+(1×1) = 9+2+1 = 12. PLA: (2×3)+(2×2)+(2×1) = 6+4+2 = 12. Cardboard: (1×3)+(3×2)+(3×1) = 3+6+3 = 12. A tie — which means my weights and ratings are saying all three are equivalent. That's data: I need to re-examine whether my weight assignment reflects what actually matters."

The key insight: "The matrix is a tool to make your reasoning explicit and check it for consistency — not a magic answer generator. A tie means I need to think harder, not just pick randomly."

Part 2  ·  Meet the Actuator: REV Smart Robot Servo (5 min)

Your One Moving Part

A servo motor is different from a DC motor: it moves to a specific position and holds it, rather than spinning continuously. You command a servo with a position value (0°–270° for the REV Smart Robot Servo) and it drives to that angle and holds. This makes it ideal for a claw: command it to 0° to open, 90° to half-open, and 180° to closed. The servo provides the force; your mechanism converts that angular motion into the gripping action you need.

Your design must use exactly one REV Smart Robot Servo as the actuating force. You may use any REV structural components — extrusions, brackets, flat plates, servo blocks, motion hardware (gears, sprockets, chain, axles, pins) — from the class inventory to build the claw mechanism itself. You must also design a mounting interface: how the claw assembly attaches to a robot arm or structure. You are not designing the arm — only the claw and its mount point.

Procedure — How Does Arm Length Change What a Servo Can Lift?

Step 1 — Short arm (3 cm). Tape the popsicle stick to the servo horn so it extends 3 cm from the center. Command the servo to hold position. Hang the coin bag from the tip. Add coins one at a time until the servo visibly strains or stalls (you'll hear it buzzing or feel it give). Count the coins and record the total mass.

Step 2 — Medium arm (6 cm). Reposition the stick to extend 6 cm. Repeat the same test with an empty bag. Add coins until the servo stalls. Record the total mass.

Step 3 — Long arm (9 cm). Extend to 9 cm and repeat. Record.

Step 4 — Predict before you analyze. Before writing anything: what pattern did you observe? What do you think will happen at 12 cm?

1. What happened to the weight the servo could hold as the arm got longer? Write a rule in your own words.

2. Your claw fingers are the arm in this experiment. If your claw isn't gripping hard enough but you can't swap the servo, what is the one change you could make to the finger length — and what would you give up by making that change? Base your answer on what you observed.

3. Look at the stall torque spec on the REV servo: 13.5 kg·cm. The "cm" part is a length. Based on what you observed in the experiment, what do you think the "cm" refers to — and why does arm length appear in a force rating?

What you discovered: The longer the arm, the less force you can apply at the tip — the servo's strength is spread over more distance. This relationship (force × distance = a constant) is called torque, and it's why the servo spec lists its strength in kg·cm rather than just kg. You just measured torque without knowing the formula — now you have the intuition behind it.

Part 3  ·  EDP Stage 1 & 2 — Define & Research (8 min) Reviews C2

What Does a Good Claw Actually Need to Do?

Class 2 connection — EDP Stage 1: Before sketching anything, define the problem precisely. What are the requirements? What are the constraints? What does success look like? Students who skip this step build the wrong thing correctly.

The claw will be used in an FTC-style game environment. It must be able to pick up and securely hold a foam cube (approximately 4 inches / ~10 cm on each side, weighing approximately 80 g). It must release the cube reliably on command. It must fit within a robot that starts inside an 18"×18" footprint and cannot extend beyond 42" height at start. It will be mounted to a robot arm (which you will design later) through a defined interface point.

Non-negotiable requirements: Uses exactly one REV Smart Robot Servo. Grips a 10 cm foam cube reliably. Releases on command. Can be removed and reattached to the arm with basic tools. Total mechanism weight should be minimized — every gram on the end of an arm multiplies the load on the arm motor.

You Do Part 3B — Linkage Experiment, then Research
Materials (per group) — 5 minutes
4 cardboard strips (~15 cm each)  ·  4 brass paper fasteners  ·  1 single cardboard strip for comparison
Procedure — How Does a Linkage Transfer Motion?

Step 1 — Single pivot (simple). Connect two strips end-to-end with one brass fastener. Hold one strip still and rotate the other. Watch the tip of the moving strip. Sketch what it does.

Step 2 — Four-strip linkage. Connect all four strips in a loop using all four fasteners (one at each corner) to make a four-sided shape. Hold one side still. Push one of the adjacent sides. Watch what the opposite side does. Sketch what you observe.

Step 3 — Compare. With the single pivot, when you push one side in, the other side does what? With the four-strip linkage, when you push one side, the opposite side does what? Which one keeps both ends more parallel to each other?

Linkage observations:

Linkage type What did the opposite end do when you pushed one side? How parallel were the two ends throughout the motion?
Single pivot
Four-strip linkage

4. If one side of the four-strip linkage were a claw finger and the other side were the opposite finger, which type of linkage would grip a cube more evenly? Why?

What you just used: The four-strip linkage is the basis of a mechanism called a 4-bar linkage — one of the most common mechanisms in engineering. You don't need to know the math to use it. What matters is what you observed: it keeps the opposite side moving in a controlled, predictable way. In your claw sketches, you can now make an informed choice about whether to use a simple single-pivot design or a more controlled 4-bar approach — because you've felt both work with your hands.

Part 5  ·  EDP Stage 3 — Ideate: Three Claw Designs with Full Orthographic Documentation (20 min)

Three Genuinely Different Mechanisms — Drawn Like an Engineer

Each design must use the REV Smart Robot Servo as the sole actuator and must fit a 10 cm foam cube. Each sketch must include: (a) a top view and a front/side profile view, (b) labeled key dimensions (finger length, max open width, max closed width, overall height), (c) the servo location and orientation marked, and (d) the mounting interface point clearly labeled. Class 3 sketching standards

I Do Watch the teacher model three genuinely different claw architectures

How I think about three DIFFERENT designs: "Different means different mechanisms — not just different shapes of the same idea. Three fundamentally different ways a servo can drive a claw: (1) direct-drive: servo horn directly connects to one jaw, the other jaw is a mirror that moves via a linkage. (2) Rack-and-pinion: servo rotates a gear that drives two opposing racks, which push the fingers inward. (3) Single-actuated passive: servo drives one active finger; the other finger is spring-loaded (passive) and is pushed open by the servo — when servo releases, spring closes it."

Design C1 — Direct-drive parallel jaw: "Front view: two flat fingers, each 8 cm long, connected at a central pivot. Top view shows fingers are 1 cm wide, spread 12 cm open at max travel. Servo mounts at the top center. The horn attaches to one finger; a link rod connects that finger to the mirror finger so they open and close symmetrically. Mounting: a flat REV plate with 4 bolt holes on the back of the servo block — any arm can bolt to this. Advantage: simple, few parts, symmetric grip. Weakness: parallel-jaw design means the fingers touch the cube only at two points — the cube can rotate/slip sideways."

Design C2 — 4-bar scissor linkage: "A crossed 4-bar linkage converts the servo's rotation into the finger closing motion. The fingers approach the cube at an angle, wrapping around it rather than squeezing from two sides. Front view shows an X-shaped linkage. Servo mounts to one pivot. Advantage: wrap-around grip is far more secure, especially for foam which compresses. Weakness: 4-bar requires precise fabrication of the link lengths — if any link is off, the mechanism binds."

Design C3 — Three-finger compliant claw: "Three fingers arranged 120° apart. Servo drives all three simultaneously through a central disk-to-finger linkage. Each finger has a rubber-tipped end for friction. Top view shows a Y-shape. When servo rotates, the disk pushes all three fingers inward. Advantage: three contact points distribute the load — the cube can't escape. Weakness: 3× the linkage complexity. Much heavier than C1 or C2."

Why I sketch before evaluating: "Notice that I don't compare these while sketching. I sketch first, then evaluate. The moment I start comparing, I stop generating new ideas — I anchor on the first design I liked."

Part 7  ·  Final Design Drawing — Notebook-Quality Entry (12 min)

Produce the Engineering Drawing for Your Selected Design

Your selected design now gets a complete, clean engineering drawing. This is the drawing someone else would use to build your claw. It must meet all Class 3 documentation standards: multiple orthographic views, labeled dimensions, title block, and revision box.

I Do Watch the teacher model a complete final engineering drawing of the claw

What a final drawing must include that a sketch doesn't: "The ideation sketches from Part 5 were rough — fast, focused on concept, with approximate dimensions. The final drawing is precise: dimensions come from actual REV part measurements, not estimates. I look up the servo horn diameter (5.5 mm hex), the bracket hole spacing (8 mm), and the extrusion width (15 mm) before drawing. Every critical dimension is a real number."

Three views minimum for a mechanism: "For the claw I draw: (1) Top view — shows finger spread from above, servo orientation, overall width. (2) Front view — shows height, finger length, how servo connects to linkage. (3) Side view — shows depth, mounting interface profile. Each view shows different information. No single view shows the whole mechanism."

Mounting interface detail: "I draw a separate, enlarged detail view of the mounting interface — how the claw assembly attaches to the arm. I label: bolt pattern (e.g., 4× M3 × 8 mm screws in an REV bracket pattern), the interface plane (the flat surface that mates against the arm), and a note: 'INTERFACE: standard REV flat bracket, 48 mm × 48 mm hole pattern.'"

Revision box — starting from scratch: "Rev 1 is today's date. The description is 'Initial design — direct-drive parallel jaw claw for 10 cm foam cube target. Three views. Interface: REV flat bracket.' My initials. If next week I change the finger length, that's Rev 2."

Part 8  ·  Notebook Entry, Cross-Lesson Analysis & Exit Reflection (7 min)

Document the Full Design Journey

Notebook Entry Requirements

Transfer your final claw drawing into your engineering notebook — the three-view drawing, the mounting interface detail, the title block, the revision box (Rev 1 = initial entry), and the parts list. Below the drawing, write a 2–3 sentence design rationale: which criterion drove your decision, what trade-off you accepted, and what you would change if you could redesign it tomorrow. Date and sign the entry.

Notebook entry checklist:

1. You used a weighted decision matrix today instead of a simple one from Class 3. Name one specific instance where the weighting changed the outcome — or, if the winner was the same, explain why you think the weights didn't change the outcome in this particular case.

2. Look at your final claw drawing. Name one dimension or design decision that was purposeful (from Class 1 vocabulary) — you had a specific engineering reason for it. Explain that reason.

Extension / Exploration — How Precise Does Your Claw Need to Be?

Try this with your group: Everyone cuts a strip of paper that is supposed to be exactly 10 cm long — freehand, no ruler. Measure each strip. Record the shortest and longest. The difference is your group's natural variation.

Now apply that to your claw design. Look at your final drawing and pick one critical dimension — the gap between the fingers when fully closed is a good choice. Ask yourself: what is the ideal measurement? Now write: "If this dimension is [ideal] ± [your acceptable range], the claw will work. If it goes outside that range, it will fail because [reason]." Try closing your fingers on the 10 cm foam cube and measuring the actual gap — does your as-built claw match your drawing? The difference between what you drew and what you built is called manufacturing variation, and understanding it is the first step toward building robots that work reliably every time.
