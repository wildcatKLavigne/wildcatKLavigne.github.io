Part 1  ·  Engineering Sketching Standards (15 min)

How Engineers Draw: Orthographic Views, Dimensions & Revision Boxes

Engineering sketches are not art — they are communication. A sketch must be clear enough that someone who was not present could build exactly what you drew. Three standards make this possible:

Orthographic views show an object straight-on from one direction — no perspective, no foreshortening. You need at least a Front view and a Top view to describe a 3D object. Complex parts also need a Right Side view. Each view is a separate flat drawing, positioned in a standard arrangement.

Dimension labels show exact measurements. Every critical length, width, and height gets a dimension line with arrows and a number. Always write units (cm, mm, or inches). Never rotate dimension numbers — always write them horizontally so they can be read without turning the page.

The title block and revision box are your documentation record. The title block (bottom-right of a sketch) records: part name, designer name, date, and scale. The revision box (above the title block) tracks every change: Rev #, date, what changed, and initials. This is how engineers prove a design evolved purposefully — not accidentally.

Sketching rules — memorize these
  • Use a ruler for all dimension lines. Freehand sketch the part; rule the annotations.
  • Write dimension numbers horizontally — never rotate them 90°.
  • Always state units. "12" with no unit is not a valid dimension.
  • Every sketch must have a date, your name, and a title. No exceptions.
  • When you change a design, add a revision entry — do not erase the original.
  • The 3-sketches rule: always sketch at least 3 alternatives before choosing one.

I Do Watch the teacher model multi-view sketching of an engineering object

Object: A standard REV Robotics flat bracket — a rectangular metal plate with 4 holes, approximately 3 cm × 6 cm × 0.2 cm.

Front view (what I draw first): "I look straight at the flat face of the bracket. I see a rectangle, 6 cm wide and 3 cm tall. I sketch the outline, then add 4 small circles for the holes at the corners. I draw dimension lines: one horizontal arrow showing 6 cm across the top, one vertical arrow showing 3 cm on the left side. I label each dimension with the number and unit."

Top view (looking straight down): "Now I imagine looking straight down on the bracket from above. I see the same rectangle — but now I understand it's flat. The thickness (0.2 cm) shows as just a thin line. This view confirms the bracket is a plate, not a cube."

Side view (looking at the thin edge): "From the side, the bracket is just a thin line — 0.2 cm thick. This view communicates the thickness, which neither the front nor top view shows clearly."

Title block and revision box: "Bottom right: 'REV Flat Bracket · [my name] · [today's date] · Scale: 2:1'. Above it, the revision box. Rev 1: [date] — Initial sketch. If I later find the hole spacing was wrong, I'd add: Rev 2: [new date] — corrected hole spacing to 2.5 cm."

Part 2  ·  Fly First, Think Second — Pre-Design Experiment (12 min)

The Paper Airplane Challenge — Two Objectives, Three Iterations

The Challenge: Using one sheet of standard 8.5"×11" paper and no tape, no scissors, and no other materials, design and fold a paper airplane. You will run two separate sub-challenges, each with three iterations of one sheet of paper per attempt:

Challenge A — Distance: Throw from a fixed launch line. Measure the distance from launch line to where the plane first touches the floor. Best of 3 throws per attempt counts.

Challenge B — Accuracy: Throw at a target (a circle of 60 cm diameter on the floor, placed 4 meters from the launch line). Score = 3 pts if the plane lands inside, 1 pt if it hits the circle edge, 0 otherwise. Total score across 3 throws per attempt.

Before you design anything, you are going to fly two pre-made planes and observe what happens. Your observations — not a textbook — will build your research. This is how engineers actually learn: watch first, theorize second.

Materials (per group)
2 pre-folded planes your teacher provides: Plane T (classic narrow dart) and Plane W (wide-wing glider)  ·  1 measuring tape  ·  1 target circle on the floor (teacher sets up)
Procedure

Step 1 — Look before you throw. Hold both planes side by side. Write down 3 physical differences you can see between Plane T and Plane W. Don't fly them yet.

Step 2 — Distance test. Each group member throws Plane T three times and records the best distance. Repeat with Plane W. Use the same throwing motion for both.

Step 3 — Accuracy test. Each group member throws Plane T three times at the target circle and records the score (3/1/0). Repeat with Plane W.

Step 4 — Predict before you analyze. Before filling in the table below, write one sentence predicting which plane will win each challenge and why — based only on what you saw and felt.

Step 4 — Analyze your results:

1. Which plane won on distance? Look back at your Step 1 observations. Which physical difference do you think caused it to fly farther? Explain.

2. Which plane was more accurate? Which physical feature do you think made it more or less predictable in flight? Explain.

3. Could one plane win both challenges? Based on what you observed, why is it hard to build a plane that goes both far AND lands accurately?

Design vocabulary from your experiment: The narrow shape of Plane T is called a high aspect ratio (long and thin relative to width) — this cuts through the air with less drag. The upward tilt of Plane W's wings is called dihedral — it makes the plane self-correct when it rolls sideways, which is why it's more predictable. The weight concentrated at Plane T's nose is called nose weight — it keeps the flight path flat and fast. You just discovered all three concepts by flying, not by reading.

Part 3  ·  Stage 3 — Ideate: Three Sketched Designs for Each Challenge (10 min)

3 Designs × 2 Objectives = 6 Orthographic Sketches

For each challenge (distance, accuracy), sketch three different paper airplane designs using proper orthographic conventions: top view and front/side profile at minimum, labeled key features, and estimated dimensions. Then evaluate with a decision matrix and select one for each challenge.

I Do Watch the teacher model three distance-plane designs with engineering sketches

How I approach three genuinely different designs: "I don't vary color or decoration — I vary the fundamental aerodynamic architecture. Three things I can fundamentally change: wing shape (aspect ratio — long/narrow vs. short/wide), nose weight distribution (heavy point vs. flat nose), and wing angle (flat vs. dihedral). Each combination gives a different plane character."

Design D1 — Classic dart (distance): Top view shows a long narrow triangle — high aspect ratio. Front view shows flat wings, sharply folded nose. Dimensions: total length ~27 cm, wingspan ~8 cm, nose angle ~20°. Advantage: cuts through air efficiently, fast and far. Weakness: sensitive to launch angle — small errors in throw angle cause large distance losses.

Design D2 — Wide-wing glider (distance): Top view shows broader delta wing — wingspan ~18 cm, length ~22 cm. Front view shows slight upward wing angle (dihedral ~15°). Advantage: more lift, glides longer even at lower speed. Weakness: needs a precise slow throw — a hard throw stalls it.

Design D3 — Weighted nose dart (distance): Top view is narrow like D1 but front multiple-fold nose concentrates weight at tip. Extra folds at the nose add ~3g of paper mass. Advantage: nose weight keeps trajectory flat, resists pitching up. Weakness: heavier, falls faster if not thrown hard enough to generate sufficient lift.

My decision logic: "I score D1=3, D2=2, D3=2 on distance potential; D1=1, D2=3, D3=2 on throw consistency; D1=2, D2=1, D3=3 on stability. D1 wins on raw potential but D3 wins on stability. For iteration 1 I choose D1 because I want to establish a performance baseline — I can always add nose weight later."

Extension / Exploration — How Precise Is Precise Enough?

Try this: Have every person in your group cut a paper strip that is exactly 10 cm long — freehand, no ruler allowed. Then measure each strip. Record the actual lengths. What is the shortest? The longest? What is the range (longest minus shortest)?

Now imagine those strips were the wings of a paper airplane and they needed to be equal length within 3 mm for the plane to fly straight. Which of your group's strips would cause a problem? In your engineering notebook, write: "For my plane to fly straight, the wingspan needs to be [your target] ± [range you think is acceptable] cm, otherwise it will fail because [reason]." That range is called a tolerance — and writing it down before you build is one of the things that separates an engineer from a guesser.
