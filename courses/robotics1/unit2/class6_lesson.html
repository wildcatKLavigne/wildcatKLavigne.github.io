Part 1  ·  The Four Engineering Team Roles (10 min)

How Engineering Teams Organize Work

In a real engineering team — and in FTC robotics competitions — no single person can do everything well at once. Teams that try to have everyone do everything end up with duplicated effort, missing documentation, and parts that don't fit together. Teams that assign clear ownership succeed more consistently.

This course uses four roles that rotate across units so every student practices every skill. The roles aren't rigid boxes — a Build Lead still takes notes, a Documentation Lead still helps assemble — but each role has a clear primary responsibility that only they are accountable for. When something goes wrong, the role owner is the first person asked: "What does your documentation say? What was the test result? What does the part count show?"

🔧Build Lead
  • Owns the physical construction — sets build sequence, assigns sub-tasks
  • Makes real-time build decisions and records the reason for each
  • Ensures the built product matches the approved design sketch
  • Calls team reviews when a deviation from the plan is needed
  • Verifies squareness, alignment, and fastener torque before sign-off

Part 2  ·  Write Your Team Charter (15 min)

The Agreement That Governs Your Team

A team charter is a written agreement that defines how your team works — not what you're building, but how you make decisions, resolve disagreements, divide responsibilities, and hold each other accountable. It is signed by every team member and referenced whenever there is a disagreement about process.

The most important moment for a team charter is not when everything is going well. It's when a teammate isn't pulling their weight, or when two people disagree about which design to build, or when the team is behind schedule and tempted to skip documentation. A charter doesn't prevent those moments — it gives the team a pre-agreed procedure for handling them without drama.

I Do Watch the teacher model facilitating a charter negotiation for one section

The decision-making norm — the hardest one to get right: "The question is: who decides when the team disagrees? There are three options. (1) Majority vote — fastest, but can leave one person feeling steamrolled. (2) The role owner decides in their domain — Build Lead decides build sequence, Documentation Lead decides notebook format, but design selection requires consensus. (3) Teacher arbitrates — a last resort, not a first resort. I recommend option 2 as the default with a 'bring it to the table' requirement: if any team member calls a 'table it,' the team stops and discusses before the owner decides. This respects both role authority and team buy-in."

The accountability norm — be specific: "Vague norms like 'everyone tries their best' are useless. Specific norms work: 'If a team member doesn't complete their role responsibility for a class, they owe the Documentation Lead a one-paragraph written explanation of what happened by the next class, added to the notebook.' That's enforceable. That's a real norm."

Role rotation — plan it now: "Decide now when roles rotate so no one is surprised. I suggest rotating every unit. That way everyone builds, everyone documents, everyone manages parts, and everyone runs tests by the end of the course. Write the rotation schedule into the charter."

Part 3  ·  The Engineering Challenge: Rotary Intake Flapper Design (40 min) All Unit 1 Skills

Design a Flapper Module for the REV Starter Bot Rotary Intake

Later in this course your team will build a rotary intake mechanism similar to the one used on the REV DUO FTC Starter Bot. A rotary intake uses a motor-driven axle that spins continuously. Flexible "flapper" elements attached to the axle sweep game elements — in your case, 10 cm foam cubes — into the robot as the axle rotates.

The flapper is a module in the modular sense you studied in Unit 1, Class 5. It attaches to the axle through a defined interface (the axle diameter and mounting pattern), can be removed and replaced without redesigning the axle or frame, and must be testable on its own before full robot integration. Your team will design, fabricate, and test at least one prototype flapper today.

Interface constraint: Your flappers must mount on a 5mm hex REV axle. The mounting mechanism (how the flapper attaches to the axle) is part of your design — use whatever REV hardware, custom fabrication, or available materials you can justify. The flapper body can be any material: REV structural parts, cardboard, foam, rubber, fabric, zip ties, pool noodle foam, or anything else readily available.

Performance objective: The flapper sweeps a 10 cm foam cube into a collection zone as the axle rotates. It must be able to do this consistently across 3 consecutive throws of the cube toward the intake. Softer, more compliant materials tend to grab game elements better; stiffer materials tend to be more durable. Both are valid trade-offs.
