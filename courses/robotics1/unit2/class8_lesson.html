Part 1  ·  What Is Risk Management? (12 min)

Seeing Problems Before They Happen

A risk is anything that might prevent your project from succeeding. Not all risks are equally dangerous. Some are very likely but have low impact. Others are unlikely but catastrophic if they occur. A risk register is a living document that lists every identified risk, scores it on likelihood (1–3) and impact (1–3), calculates a priority score (likelihood × impact), and assigns a mitigation strategy.

A mitigation strategy is what you do before the risk happens to reduce its likelihood or impact. "Hope it doesn't happen" is not a mitigation strategy. "Design a second mounting interface so we can swap the mechanism in under 5 minutes if the primary mount fails" is a mitigation strategy.

Risk management is not pessimism — it is intentional design applied to the project itself. Just as you design a claw with a trade-off between grip force and weight, you design your project plan with deliberate choices about which risks to eliminate, which to reduce, and which to accept. You can only manage risks you have named first.

I Do Watch the teacher model identifying and scoring risks for a chassis build

How I identify risks: "I ask: 'What could prevent us from completing this task on time and with good quality?' For the chassis build, I think through every step: design, parts, build, test. Design risk: we spend too long debating which design to use and don't leave enough time to build. Parts risk: a key bracket isn't in our stockpile and we can't source it quickly. Build risk: the chassis isn't square and we don't catch it until after wiring. Test risk: the first drive reveals a gear ratio problem that requires a complete rebuild."

Scoring and prioritizing: "I score Likelihood 1 (unlikely) to 3 (very likely) and Impact 1 (inconvenient) to 3 (project-threatening). Priority = L × I. Score 7–9: critical — address immediately. Score 4–6: significant — have a mitigation plan. Score 1–3: monitor only. The chassis-not-square risk: Likelihood=2 (we've seen this before), Impact=3 (have to redo mounting). Priority = 6. Significant. Mitigation: Quality Lead measures diagonals before any motors are installed."

The risk register is a living document: "When a risk becomes real — when the chassis isn't square — the Documentation Lead updates the risk register: 'Risk materialized Class 11. Mitigation implemented: rebuilt frame. Result: chassis now within spec. 1 class period lost.' This becomes part of the notebook's design history."

Part 3  ·  Team Communication: Status Updates & Stand-Ups (20 min)

Raising Problems Early Enough to Solve Them

The most common reason engineering teams fail isn't bad engineering — it's communication. A team member knows a part is missing and assumes someone else is handling it. A sub-assembly isn't going to fit and nobody says anything until build day. A team member is confused about their role responsibility and spends two classes doing the wrong task.

Two tools prevent these failures. The stand-up meeting (practiced in Classes 6 and 7) is a 3-minute daily check-in: what did I do, what am I doing next, what is blocking me. The status update is a written paragraph submitted at the end of each unit that communicates progress to the teacher and stakeholders. Both tools have one purpose: surface problems early, before they compound.

Effective status updates use a traffic light system: 🟢 Green = on track, 🟡 Yellow = at risk / needs attention, 🔴 Red = blocked / off track. A Red status is not failure — it is honest engineering. What fails teams is a Red status that nobody reports until it's too late to fix.

I Do Watch the teacher write a model status update — good and bad versions

Bad status update (vague, no action, no accountability): "We are working on the chassis. It's going okay. We might be a bit behind. We'll try to catch up next class." This tells a reader nothing they can act on. What is the specific problem? Who owns the recovery? What is the new plan?

Good status update (specific, with action items and owner): "Status: 🟡 Yellow. The chassis frame was completed in Class 11, but the squareness test failed — diagonals were off by 8mm. Root cause: the corner bracket on the front-left was installed 90° rotated. Action taken: rebuilt the corner joint in Class 11, re-tested, now within 2mm tolerance. Impact to schedule: 0.5 class periods lost from the buffer allocation in Phase 3. Updated milestone: drivetrain wiring now targets Class 12 instead of Class 11. No impact to overall completion date. Owner: Build Lead."

Why specificity matters: "The good update gives the reader everything they need to understand the situation without asking follow-up questions. The bad update forces the teacher to stop class and interview the team. Specific status updates respect everyone's time and demonstrate engineering professionalism."
